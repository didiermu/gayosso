Apps on Foldable Devices

Foldable phones are bursting onto the scene, promising a whole new playground for app designers.

Adopting a trend  too late is like showing up to a party when the pizza's all gone— not good enough, especially when we're talking about a true shift in paradigm. Foldable phones are bursting onto the scene, promising a whole new playground for app designers. It's a no-brainer for the fresh-faced apps, but for those apps tethered to legacy design patterns and code, the thought of jumping on the foldable bandwagon can be scarier and harder than it seems. The form factor of foldable phones throws up challenges left and right, but amidst the uncertainty of whether it's worth diving in headfirst, there's a treasure trove of opportunities for those brave enough to take the plunge. Trust me, it's not about if it'll be worth it, but rather when and how to start the foldable fiesta.

First things first: flexible layouts are key. Unlike your run-of-the-mill square smartphones, foldable devices introduce variability in screen size and orientation. UX designers must adapt their designs to accommodate these changes seamlessly, ensuring a consistent and optimized experience across different configurations.


Moreover, the manner in which users interact with foldable devices differs from traditional smartphones. Factors such as reachability variants depending on the state must be taken into account to enhance usability.

App continuity is like the glue holding it all together. Imagine folding and unfolding your device like it's a high-stakes game of origami—your app should seamlessly transition between states without leaving users feeling like they've been thrown down a rabbit hole.


And don't even get me started on fragmentation. It's like trying to wrangle a herd of cats; there's just no telling what each device will bring to the table. But hey, where there's chaos, there's opportunity. UX designers could optimize content consumption for micro-interactions, leveraging the available screen real estate efficiently. This involves striking a balance between information density and usability, ensuring that users can access relevant content without feeling overwhelmed, even if there is a much larger screen. What would the user gain by seeing all possible actions from our app at once? In this context, how might we anticipate and adapt to their needs?

“It's like trying to wrangle a herd of cats; there's just no telling what each device will bring to the table”.

Furthermore, different screen sizes, which encompass smaller dimensions, offer new possibilities for micro-usage; therefore, widgets, notifications, and quick contextual actions will be even more relevant.

Navigation is a whole other beast. Do you stick with a traditional persistent or partially persistent menus, or do you go for broke with a navigation hub catering task-oriented interactions? It's a tough call, but whatever you choose, just make sure it keeps users from throwing their devices out the window in frustration.

Foldable devices blur the line between mobile and tablet, with split-screen and multi-window modes enabling concurrent activities, opens up a world of possibilities for multitasking mavens. However, this may potentially lead to decreased attention rates and, in fields such as medical devices, that's a prescription for disaster.
