Apps on Foldable Devices

Foldable phones are bursting onto the scene, promising a whole new playground for app designers.

Adopting a trend  too late is like showing up to a party when the pizza's all gone— not good enough, especially when we're talking about a true shift in paradigm. Foldable phones are bursting onto the scene, promising a whole new playground for app designers and developers. It's a no-brainer for the fresh-faced apps, but for those apps tethered to legacy design patterns and code, the thought of jumping on the foldable universe can be scarier and harder than it seems. The form factor of foldable phones throws up challenges left and right, but amidst the uncertainty of whether it's worth diving in headfirst, there's a treasure trove of opportunities for those brave enough to take the plunge. Trust me, it's not about if it'll be worth it, but rather when and how to start the foldable fiesta.

First things first: flexible layouts are key. Unlike your run-of-the-mill square smartphones, foldable devices introduce variability in screen size and orientation. UX designers and developers must think and adapt their designs to accommodate these changes seamlessly, ensuring a consistent and optimized experience across different configurations.


Moreover, the manner in which users interact with foldable devices differs from traditional smartphones. Factors such as reachability variants depending on the state must be taken into account to enhance usability.

App continuity is like the glue holding it all together. Imagine folding and unfolding your device like it's a high-stakes game of origami—your app should seamlessly transition between states without leaving users feeling like they've been thrown down a rabbit hole.


And don't even get me started on fragmentation. There's just no telling what each device will bring to the table. But hey, where there's chaos, there's opportunity. UX designers could optimize content consumption for micro-interactions, leveraging the available screen real estate efficiently. This involves striking a balance between information density and usability, ensuring that users can access relevant content without feeling overwhelmed, even if there is a much larger screen. What would the user gain by seeing all possible actions from our app at once? In this context, how might we anticipate and adapt to their needs?

Furthermore, different screen sizes, which encompass smaller dimensions, offer new possibilities for micro-usage; therefore, widgets, notifications, and quick contextual actions will be even more relevant.

Navigation is a whole other beast. Do you stick with traditional persistent or partially persistent menus, or do you go for broke with a navigation hub catering task-oriented interactions? It's a tough call, but whatever you choose, just make sure it keeps users from throwing their devices out the window in frustration.

As foldable devices blur the line between mobile and tablet, with split-screen and multi-window modes enabling concurrent activities, a new world of possibilities for multitasking mavens is open. However, this potentially leads to decreased attention rates and, in fields such as medical devices, introduces a new risk to evaluate, as well as a new analysis framework.

In conclusion, diving into foldable devices is like deciding to build a new app altogether, let's be prepared to challenge assumptions and previously made decisions, hand in hand with your stakeholders. It's thrilling, it's terrifying, and it's bound to leave us feeling a little queasy at times. I believe the key lies in seizing the moment and charting a course where quick gains are evident, but there is continual progress towards a refined and truly fold-aware app. So let’s roll up our sleeves and get to work, because the only way to navigate this is to jump right in and hold on tight to the business strategy.
