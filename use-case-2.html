Use case

Rethinking Performance Management

COMPANY

TalentGuard

INDUSTRY

Human Resources

DURATION

2 Months

ROLE PLAYED

UX Design Practitioner and Manager

Shifting Requirements with Hypotheses

Introduction

TalentGuard, a provider of HR software solutions, encountered usability challenges within its Performance Management suite, and their developer-centric approach was not helping. With the sudden absence of a Product Manager, the UX design team seized the opportunity to intervene earlier, reframing stakeholder requirements as hypotheses and conducting initial studies to validate them.

Outcome

Embracing a hypothesis-driven approach and utilizing remote collaborative workshops, TalentGuard significantly enhanced the user experience in its core module. This approach nurtured internal trust in generative design processes, fostering smoother collaboration and decision-making. Consequently, it led to a more inclusive roadmap that allocated time for UX activities.

Process

The UX team at TalentGuard embarked on a comprehensive journey by reframing requirement as a hypothesis, within the realm of Service Design methodologies. With a simple tweak:

Requirement:

"The performance management module must have a dashboard displaying metrics A, B and C."

Hypothesis::

â€œDelivering key metrics that users consider critical through dynamic visualization has the potential to boost user engagement and improve decision-making within the performance management module."

By reframing the requirement as hypothesis, the UX team conducted user research to validate whether the proposed solution truly improved user engagement and decision-making, pivoting and exploring alternative solutions as needed without being constrained by the initial requirement.

Beginning with benchmark research and contextual inquiries to grasp user needs and challenges, the team created artifacts including a service blueprint to map the user journey and facilitate collaborative alignment. Engaging all stakeholders, particularly the developerâ€™s lead (who later became an UX advocate), was critical as the UX value wasn't always recognized, and the organization was still validating its impact. In the middle of a global pandemic, remote workshops using Miro enabled inclusive participation and ideation across teams.

Managing stakeholdersâ€™ expectations played a pivotal role, with sessions focused on prioritizing issues and assessing risks and efforts. The UX design team concentrated on fostering empathy for users and generating solutions, involving stakeholders beyond the development team. Through iterative sessions, actionable items were identified and categorized based on their impact and effort, guiding future design decisions.

Conclusion:

This case study illustrates the transformative power of reframing requirements as hypotheses and empowering design within Agile product management. The service blueprint emerged as a critical artifact that resonated with all stakeholders and proved instrumental in both design and development. Other fundamental practices such as decision frameworks, collaborative protocols, trust, and confidence in the process were crucial in overcoming challenges and fostering innovation.

In summary, this project not only addressed immediate usability concerns but also laid the groundwork for a culture of design-led innovation within TalentGuard. By submitting this case study to the ACM CHI Conference 2021, the UX Team at TalentGuard aimed to share its journey and inspire others in the field of Human-Computer Interaction.
